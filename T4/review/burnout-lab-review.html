P01 · legs

Quad Lockdown

STATUS: APPROVE: Ready for in-person pilot testing

20-second explanation: Ten thirty-second blocks. Move immediately at each call. Regress at the first loss of the stated form standard and keep working cleanly.

  1. 0:00–0:30 Wall sit at 90-degree angle (knees bent to right angle, back against wall).
  2. 0:30–1:00 Controlled bodyweight squats at moderate pace.
  3. 1:00–1:30 Wall sit at 90-degree angle, sustained hold.
  4. 1:30–2:00 Bodyweight squats at controlled pace (replace lunges for safety at fatigue point).
  5. 2:00–2:30 Wall sit at 90-degree angle, hold.
  6. 2:30–3:00 Squat pulses in bottom third of range (short-lever work when fatigued).
  7. 3:00–3:30 Wall sit at 90-degree angle (shallower recovery hold if needed).
  8. 3:30–4:00 Fast bodyweight squats while maintaining controlled depth and knee tracking; regress to moderate pace if form begins to slip.
  9. 4:00–4:30 Squat pulses in bottom third of range (high-rep finish work).
  10. 4:30–5:00 Squat pulses until unable to maintain control; hold in bottom position for final 5-10 seconds.
Precise effect
quadriceps and glute muscular endurance
Technical-failure stop
Knees stop tracking over toes, hips shift off wall, torso collapses, or depth becomes uncontrolled.
Immediate regression
Lower wall sit to shallower angle; reduce squat depth to waist-height level; slow all movements to controlled pace.
Progression
Maintain 90-degree wall sit depth; increase squat cadence; extend pulse-work duration.
Why this beats one exercise for five minutes
90-degree wall sits create sustained isometric load that pre-fatigues quadriceps. Following dynamic squats and pulses then demand high motor-unit recruitment when muscles are already compromised. The rhythm—sit, move, sit, move—maintains fatigue while allowing brief motion-recovery in different planes.
Preceding-class conflict / when not to use
Avoid after high-volume knees, kicks, squat jumps, or leg-endurance work that already approached quad technical failure.
Frequency
Use one or two times weekly; leave 48 hours before repeating the same primary tissue target.
Evidence classification

P02 · legs

Posterior Chain Melt

20-second explanation: Ten thirty-second blocks. Move immediately at each call. Regress at the first loss of the stated form standard and keep working cleanly.

  1. 0:00–0:30 Glute bridges with a two-second squeeze.
  2. 0:30–1:00 Bridge march with level hips.
  3. 1:00–1:30 Glute bridges at a steady cadence.
  4. 1:30–2:00 Left-leg bridge; right foot stays lightly planted for assistance.
  5. 2:00–2:30 Right-leg bridge; left foot stays lightly planted for assistance.
  6. 2:30–3:00 Bridge march with small controlled steps.
  7. 3:00–3:30 Prone alternating arm and leg lifts.
  8. 3:30–4:00 Glute bridge pulses near full extension.
  9. 4:00–4:30 Glute bridge hold; breathe without arching the back.
  10. 4:30–5:00 Glute bridges; shorten range only when full extension fails.
Precise effect
glutes, hamstrings, and posterior trunk endurance
Technical-failure stop
Hip extension shifts into lumbar arching, pelvis rotates, or hamstring cramp prevents clean motion.
Immediate regression
Use two-leg bridges and smaller marches throughout.
Progression
Remove assisting-foot pressure during single-leg blocks.
Why this beats one exercise for five minutes
Bilateral work builds fatigue before assisted unilateral work; trunk extension interrupts local cramping, then pulses and a hold finish the same chain safely.
Preceding-class conflict / when not to use
Avoid after hamstring-intensive clinch, deadlift, or posterior-chain work.
Frequency
Use one or two times weekly; leave 48 hours before repeating the same primary tissue target.

P03 · upper body

Push-up Mechanical Descent

20-second explanation: Ten thirty-second blocks. Move immediately at each call. Regress at the first loss of the stated form standard and keep working cleanly.

  1. 0:00–0:30 Strict push-ups at a repeatable depth.
  2. 0:30–1:00 High-plank shoulder taps with quiet hips.
  3. 1:00–1:30 Close-grip push-ups.
  4. 1:30–2:00 High-plank hold with elbows fully controlled.
  5. 2:00–2:30 Strict push-ups; switch to knee push-ups at technical failure.
  6. 2:30–3:00 Knee push-ups at full available depth.
  7. 3:00–3:30 High-plank shoulder taps.
  8. 3:30–4:00 Close-grip knee push-ups.
  9. 4:00–4:30 High-plank hold; drop knees only when trunk position fails.
  10. 4:30–5:00 Knee push-ups; finish with a high-plank hold when repetitions fail.
Precise effect
chest, triceps, shoulders, and anterior trunk endurance
Technical-failure stop
Chest depth shortens twice, elbows flare uncontrollably, or hips sag or pike.
Immediate regression
Move immediately to knee push-ups or knee plank.
Progression
Add a three-second lowering phase to strict repetitions.
Why this beats one exercise for five minutes
The sequence descends from harder pushing to knee-supported pushing while plank blocks keep the same tissues loaded, so output continues without grinding failed strict repetitions.
Preceding-class conflict / when not to use
Avoid after high-volume punching, push-ups, shoulder injury, or wrist irritation.
Frequency
Use one or two times weekly; leave 48 hours before repeating the same primary tissue target.

P04 · upper body

Shoulder Shelf

20-second explanation: Ten thirty-second blocks. Move immediately at each call. Regress at the first loss of the stated form standard and keep working cleanly.

  1. 0:00–0:30 Fast straight shadow punches; every hand returns to guard.
  2. 0:30–1:00 High-guard isometric hold with fists at eyebrow height.
  3. 1:00–1:30 Alternating shadow hooks with complete guard return.
  4. 1:30–2:00 High-plank shoulder taps with quiet hips.
  5. 2:00–2:30 Fast straight shadow punches.
  6. 2:30–3:00 High-guard hold with small controlled fist pulses.
  7. 3:00–3:30 Alternating straight punches from a quarter squat.
  8. 3:30–4:00 Knee-plank shoulder taps.
  9. 4:00–4:30 Fast mixed straight punches and hooks.
  10. 4:30–5:00 High-guard hold; lower hands briefly only before posture fails.
Precise effect
deltoids, triceps, upper back, and postural endurance
Technical-failure stop
Hands stop returning to guard, wrists lose alignment, shoulders shrug painfully, or plank trunk control fails.
Immediate regression
Slow the punches, stand taller, and use knee-plank taps.
Progression
Increase clean punch cadence without adding impact or weights.
Why this beats one exercise for five minutes
Punching creates dynamic shoulder fatigue, guard holds remove momentum, and supported taps load the shoulder girdle without asking for more striking impact.
Preceding-class conflict / when not to use
Avoid after shoulder-heavy pad rounds, elbow pain, or compromised punch mechanics.
Frequency
Use one or two times weekly; leave 48 hours before repeating the same primary tissue target.

  5. 2:00–2:30 Slow mountain climbers with level hips.
  6. 2:30–3:00 Forearm plank with alternating foot lifts.

Lengthen levers while keeping breathing and pelvic control.

The trunk changes planes before one position degrades; dead bugs unload the shoulders while preserving abdominal tension, then the final plank retests control.

P06 · trunk

Brace and Breathe

20-second explanation: Ten thirty-second blocks. Move immediately at each call. Regress at the first loss of the stated form standard and keep working cleanly.

  1. 0:00–0:30 Alternating dead bugs.
  2. 0:30–1:00 Bear-plank hold with knees hovering low.
  3. 1:00–1:30 Slow mountain climbers.
  4. 1:30–2:00 Prone alternating arm and leg lifts.
  5. 2:00–2:30 Dead bugs with a three-second extension.
  6. 2:30–3:00 Bear-plank shoulder taps.
  7. 3:00–3:30 Slow mountain climbers with full exhale on each drive.
  8. 3:30–4:00 Prone swimmer hold; switch limbs every five seconds.
  9. 4:00–4:30 Dead bugs at the longest clean lever.
  10. 4:30–5:00 Bear-plank hold; set knees down before the spine changes.
Precise effect
deep abdominal, shoulder-stabilizer, and posterior-trunk endurance
Technical-failure stop
Spine position changes, breath is held involuntarily, or hips rotate during support work.
Immediate regression
Keep knees down in bear work and shorten dead-bug levers.
Progression
Increase hover time and limb-lever length without faster movement.
Why this beats one exercise for five minutes
Supine, supported, and prone positions alternate the direction of trunk demand; shoulders receive incomplete recovery without forcing five minutes in one plank.
Preceding-class conflict / when not to use
Avoid with wrist pain or after trunk work that already produced cramping.
Frequency
Use one or two times weekly; leave 48 hours before repeating the same primary tissue target.

P07 · total body cardio

Impact-to-Engine Drop

20-second explanation: Ten thirty-second blocks. Move immediately at each call. Regress at the first loss of the stated form standard and keep working cleanly.

  1. 0:00–0:30 Squat jumps with quiet, balanced landings.
  2. 0:30–1:00 Fast controlled bodyweight squats.
  3. 1:00–1:30 Squat thrusts with a rigid plank.
  4. 1:30–2:00 High-knee running; switch to marching before posture fails.
  5. 2:00–2:30 Squat jumps; use fast squats after the first poor landing.
  6. 2:30–3:00 Step-back squat thrusts.
  7. 3:00–3:30 High-knee running with quiet feet.
  8. 3:30–4:00 Alternating reverse lunges at a fast clean cadence.
  9. 4:00–4:30 Squat thrusts; step back as needed.
  10. 4:30–5:00 Fast bodyweight squats; finish with a wall-free squat hold if repetitions fail.
Precise effect
total-body cardiovascular output and leg endurance
Technical-failure stop
Landings become loud, knees collapse inward, plank sags, or upright running posture fails.
Immediate regression
Remove jumps, step through thrusts, and march instead of run.
Progression
Preserve impact quality longer before taking each regression.
Why this beats one exercise for five minutes
Explosive work appears only while landing quality is fresh; the sequence deliberately drops to lower-impact versions so heart rate and leg output continue after jumping is no longer safe.
Preceding-class conflict / when not to use
Avoid after impact-heavy kicking, jumping, calf pain, or Achilles irritation.
Frequency
Use one or two times weekly; leave 48 hours before repeating the same primary tissue target.

  5. 2:00–2:30 Straight punches from a quarter squat.
  6. 2:30–3:00 Bodyweight squats with hands staying at guard.
